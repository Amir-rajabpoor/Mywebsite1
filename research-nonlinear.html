Research

Nonlinear and amplitude dependent effects

It is important to understand how nonlinear interactions and finite amplitudes modify dispersion, localization, and energy transfer in mechanical and phononic lattices. In this research, the effect of nonlinearity in a nonlocal spring on wave propagation in a mass-in-mass chain with nonlocal interaction is studied.

Nonlinear mass-in-mass chains

I work with discrete chains that combine local resonators and nonlinear springs to study emerging wave phenomena and frequency shifts.

  • Models with both linear and nonlinear components in nearest-neighbor springs.
  • Extension to chains with second-neighbor (nonlocal) interactions and internal resonators.
  • Response under wave-packet excitation and periodic forcing.

Analytical approaches

Where possible I use perturbation and multiple-scale methods to obtain approximate dispersion relations and amplitude–frequency relationships.

  • Separation of fast oscillations and slow envelope evolution.
  • Derivation of effective equations for envelope dynamics.
  • Comparison between analytical predictions and numerical time integration.

Role in applications

Understanding nonlinear effects is important for both design and reliability.

  • Predicting frequency shifts and bifurcations in small-scale devices.
  • Controlling shock mitigation and energy absorption in architected materials.
  • Identifying operating ranges where nonlinearity is beneficial or harmful.
Spatio-temporal modulation

Wave excitation in a nonlinear mass-in-mass chain with nonlocal interaction

The clips below show spatio-temporal modulation of excitation waves in a nonlinear mass-in-mass chain with nonlocal springs. By choosing different central wavenumbers κ and frequencies, we selectively excite acoustic and optic branches in both linear and nonlinear regimes, and observe changes in group velocity and the emergence of solitary waves.

Acoustic branch

Acoustic-branch excitations illustrating how nonlocal nonlinearity changes dispersion and group velocity.

Linear acoustic branch

Excitation targeting the linear acoustic branch at the κ and frequency indicated in the video. The packet follows the linear dispersion, with relatively slow group velocity at higher wavenumbers.

Slow group velocity at high κ.

Nonlinear acoustic branch

Excitation of the nonlinear acoustic branch at lower wavenumber. Nonlocal nonlinearity leads to faster group velocity compared to the linear case.

Fast group velocity at lower κ.

Nonlinear acoustic branch with solitary waves

Nonlinear excitation where solitary-wave-like structures emerge and propagate through the chain.

Fast group velocity and solitary waves at lower κ.

Optic branch

Optic-branch excitations comparing linear and nonlinear responses, their group velocities, and solitary-wave formation.

Linear optic branch

Excitation of the linear optic branch at the indicated κ and frequency. The group velocity is relatively slow, but higher than the corresponding linear acoustic case at high wavenumber.

Slow group velocity, but faster than acoustic at high κ.

Nonlinear optic branch

Nonlinear excitation of the optic branch at lower wavenumber. Nonlocal nonlinear effects lead to a significantly faster group velocity.

Fast group velocity at lower κ.

Nonlinear optic branch with solitary waves

Stronger nonlinear excitation where solitary-wave-like structures appear in the spatio-temporal response.

Fast group velocity and solitary waves at lower κ.
